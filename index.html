A Visualization on

Tourist Ecosystem in Malaysia

This visualization provides an interactive overview of Malaysia's tourism landscape
in 2023/2024, showcasing nationwide trends such as top arrivals by
country, to state-level perspective on hotel supply, and top visitor nationalities,
enabling deeper insights into Malaysia's evolving tourism ecosystem.

Justin Tan | 19 September 2025

From Singapore to China: Malaysia's Top Tourist Origins

Malaysia continues to attract millions of international visitors each year, with neighbouring countries like Singapore, Indonesia, and Thailand leading the arrivals. In 2024, arrivals from Singapore surpassed 18 million, reflecting strong cross-border tourism ties and Malaysia's role as a regional hub. While short-haul travellers dominate, long-haul visitors from China, India, and the United Kingdom have also grown sharply, supported by new flight routes and visa-friendly policies.

Domestic tourism remains robust, with Malaysians significantly contributing to hotel stays across states from Kuala Lumpur to Sabah. As the country expands its cultural and eco-tourism offerings, industry analysts predict sustained growth in both arrivals and hotel occupancy rates, highlighting Malaysia's resilient and evolving tourism landscape.

Malaysia's Foreign Tourism Surges to New Heights

The line chart on the left shows monthly foreign tourist arrivals to Malaysia for 2023 and 2024, with each month in 2024 surpassing the previous year. Mid-year months from May to August see particularly strong gains, and December records the highest arrivals at over 3.8 million, reflecting peak travel season driven by holidays and year-end tourism.

The bar chart on the right highlights year-over-year growth rates, peaking in June at 49.2% and remaining strong in August at 42.1%, indicating a significant rebound in foreign travel demand. Growth moderates in the latter months, with September and November showing the lowest increases at 19.2% and 18.4%, respectively. Together, the charts provide a clear view of Malaysia's foreign tourism scale and momentum in 2024, offering insights for planning in the tourism, hospitality, and transportation sectors.

Air and Land Dominate Foreign Arrivals

The bar chart shows foreign tourist arrivals to Malaysia by mode of transport in 2023 and 2024. Air travel remains dominant, carrying nearly 11 million visitors in 2024—a 40.3% increase from the previous year—while land transport accounts for over 25 million arrivals, growing 29.5%.

Sea travel grew moderately by 13% to around 1.3 million, whereas rail travel declined slightly by 4.4%, representing just 1.5% of arrivals. Overall, Malaysia welcomed nearly 38 million foreign tourists in 2024, with air and land transport together making up over 94% of arrivals, providing valuable insights for tourism, transport, and infrastructure planning.

Foreign Tourists Flood Malaysia's Checkpoints

The proportional symbol map shows foreign tourist arrivals across Malaysia's major entry points, with circle sizes representing visitor numbers. Airports in the West, especially KLIA, KLIA2, and Tambak Johor, dominate, with KLIA and KLIA2 together handling over 8.6 million arrivals and Tambak Johor recording more than 14 million visitors.

Secondary entry points like Gelang Patah, Sungai Tujuh, and LTA Bayan Lepas also see substantial traffic, while smaller checkpoints such as Stulang Laut and Pos Kawalan Imm Muar handle fewer visitors. The map highlights the concentration of arrivals in the West and provides insights for infrastructure planning, resource allocation, and tourism promotion.

Tourism Hotspots Across Malaysia In 2023: West vs East

The choropleth map shows foreign tourist arrivals across Malaysian states, with darker shades indicating higher numbers. West Malaysia dominates, led by Johor, Kedah, Kuala Lumpur, and Selangor. Johor receives the most visitors, mainly from Thailand and Singapore, while Kedah attracts tourists from Finland, Singapore, and Saudi Arabia. Kuala Lumpur draws a diverse mix from South and West Asia, reflecting its role as the political and economic hub.

Other states like Selangor, Melaka, Pahang, and Pulau Pinang also see significant arrivals, with visitor origins varying by state. In contrast, East Malaysia records more moderate arrivals, with Sabah and Sarawak attracting mainly tourists from China, Taiwan, South Korea, and Brunei. The map highlights the concentration of foreign tourism in the West and provides insights for regional tourism planning and infrastructure development.

The first bar chart shows foreign tourist expenditure in Malaysia for 2024 compared to 2023, broken down by category. Shopping remains the largest segment at 37.4%, followed by accommodation (18.1%) and food & beverages (16.1%). International airfares grew 129.9%, reflecting increased long-haul travel, while fuel expenditure surged over 300%, highlighting rising transportation costs and tourist mobility. Other categories such as local transport, medical, organised tours, and domestic airfares also grew, though entertainment declined by 16.1%.

The second chart presents the top 20 countries contributing to Malaysia's tourist receipts. Singapore leads with over 27.9 billion MYR in 2024, followed by China at 20.9 billion MYR and Indonesia at 15.3 billion MYR. Other notable contributors include India, Thailand, Brunei, and South Korea. Together, the charts provide insights into tourist origins and spending patterns, supporting targeted marketing, tourism promotion, and policy planning.

Where Malaysia Stays: Hotel Guests and Occupancy

The choropleth map shows hotel guest counts across Malaysian states in 2024, with darker shades indicating higher totals. Kuala Lumpur leads with over 21.9 million guests, followed by Pahang, Johor, and Selangor. West Malaysia dominates overall, reflecting strong urban and tourism hubs, while East Malaysia sees moderate numbers, with Sabah and Sarawak recording over 7 million guests each.

The map also highlights hotel occupancy rates, with Pahang topping at 75.6%, Kuala Lumpur at 62.6%, and Pulau Pinang at 56.8%, while Labuan records the lowest at 37%. By combining guest volume and occupancy, the visualization provides a clear view of Malaysia’s hospitality sector, showing where tourist activity is concentrated and where hotel resources are most effectively used, informing tourism planning and infrastructure development.

From Exchange Rates to Arrivals: A 2-Year View

The line chart tracks the relationship between the U.S. dollar to Malaysian ringgit (USD/MYR) exchange rate and monthly foreign tourist arrivals from January 2023 through December 2024. The ringgit steadily weakened through much of 2023, with the exchange rate hovering around 4.5 to 4.8 per dollar, while tourist arrivals fluctuated more sharply, rising above 3 million in mid-2023 before dipping at the start of 2024.

By mid-2024, arrivals surged, peaking around 4.5 million in August, even as the currency remained under pressure. The data suggests that visitor inflows were resilient despite currency volatility, possibly even benefitting from a weaker ringgit that made Malaysia more attractive to foreign travelers. The steady climb in arrivals through 2024, despite a persistently weak ringgit, demonstrates Malaysia's growing appeal as a destination and the resilience of its tourism sector.
