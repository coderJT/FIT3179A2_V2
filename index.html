Foreign Visitors Visualisation

INTRODUCTION:

Tourism plays a vital role in Malaysia's economy, reflecting the
country's vibrant culture, natural beauty, and global appeal. This dashboard
presents an in-depth look at Malaysia's tourism landscape, exploring
who visits, how they travel, and where they go.

Through data-driven insights on visitor arrivals, spending, accommodation, and regional distribution, this visualization aims to provide a clearer understanding
of tourism trends and their impact on Malaysia's growth.

Visitor Overview

1
 Where does Malaysia's
Visitors Come From?

Malaysia continues to attract millions of international each year, with neighbouring countries like Singapore, Indonesia and Thailand leading the arrivals. During 2024, arrivals from Singapore surpassed 18 million, reflecting strong cross-border tourism ties and Malaysia's role as a regional hub.

While short-haul travellers dominate, long-haul visitors from China, India, and the United Kingdom have also grown sharply, supported by new flight routes and visa-friendly policies.

2
 Air and Land Dominate Foreign
Visitor Arrivals

Malaysia's 2024 visitor data shows a nation on the move by land, air, and sea. Land crossings dominate, led by Johor's Tambak Johor with over 14 million arrivals from Singapore. Air travel remains vital, with KLIA and KLIA 2 welcoming over 8 million visitors, alongside key airports in Penang, Kota Kinabalu, and Johor Bahru. Sea routes, though smaller, connect through ports like Stulang Laut and Pelabuhan Klang. Rail have the lowest visitor, mainly through Woodlands Train Checkpoint.

3
 Tourist Entries by
Entry Point State and Month

Johor stands out overwhelmingly, recording nearly 50 million entries, followed by Selangor and Sarawak, reflecting their roles as Malaysia's main international and regional gateways. The gender breakdown shows a relatively balanced mix across most states, with slightly higher male arrivals at key crossings such
as Johor. The timeline chart below reveals a sharp decline
in early 2020, coinciding with global travel restrictions, followed by a steady recovery beginning in 2022 and sustained growth through 2024 - a clear indicator of Malaysia's tourism rebound and reopening momentum.

Tourism Activity

4
 Tourism Hotspots Across
Malaysia In 2023

The visualization highlights Malaysia's top tourist states and their main visitor sources. The packed bubble chart shows Johor as the leading destination, followed by Kedah, Kuala Lumpur, and Selangor. Smaller states like Perlis and Sabah record lower counts. The interactive bar chart updates based on the selected state. Here, Johor reveals Thailand and Singapore as its top visitor origins, followed by other ASEAN nations, Indonesia, and Vietnam. Together, the charts illustrate Malaysia's key tourism hubs and their strongest regional travel links.

Regional Insights

5
 Where Malaysia Stays:
Hotel Guests and Occupancy

Hotel occupancy across Malaysia paints a varied landscape. Kuala Lumpur leads with strong gains in foreign guests, boosting overall occupancy, while Putrajaya and Pulau Pinang also see significant growth. Domestic demand drives notable increases in Kedah and Johor, though some regions, like Perak, Perlis, and Labuan, experience declines. Overall, the figures reveal a recovering hospitality sector, propelled by international travelers and selective domestic growth, yet uneven across states.

Trends & Economics

6
 Expenditure Breakdown
of Visitors

Tourist spending reveals a clear hierarchy of priorities. Basic needs like accommodation and dining total over $36,000, while leisure, led by shopping at nearly $40,000, dominates discretionary spending. Travel costs reflect the backbone of journeys, and smaller outlays on medical services, tours, fuel, and other expenses hint at the unpredictable realities of life on the road. The data paints travelers as pragmatic in necessities, eager in pleasures, and mindful of the journey itself.

7
 From Exchange Rates to Arrivals:
A 2-Year View

Foreign tourist arrivals in Malaysia proved surprisingly resilient despite a weakening ringgit. Through much of 2023, the USD/MYR rate hovered between 4.5 and 4.8, while arrivals fluctuated, topping 3 million mid-year before dipping early 2024. By August 2024, visitor numbers surged to 4.5 million, suggesting that a softer currency may have boosted Malaysia's appeal. The steady climb in arrivals underscores the country's growing magnetism for travelers and the robustness of its tourism sector.
